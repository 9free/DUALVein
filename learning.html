Foundational Knowledge

Vein Anatomy & Physiology

Key concepts every nursing student must understand before attempting IV insertion. Mastering anatomy dramatically improves first-attempt success rates.

Key Vein Sites for IV Access

  • Median cubital vein — antecubital fossa; first choice for most IV insertions
  • Cephalic vein — lateral forearm; good secondary site
  • Basilic vein — medial forearm; avoid near the elbow due to nerve proximity
  • Dorsal metacarpal veins — back of hand; suitable for short-term access

Sites to Avoid

  • Joints and flexion areas (increases dislodgement risk)
  • Veins with visible valves (resistance to insertion)
  • Scarred, bruised, or thrombosed veins
  • Dominant arm unless necessary
  • Areas of prior phlebitis or infiltration

Veins vs. Arteries

  • Veins have thinner walls and are more compressible
  • Veins contain valves; arteries do not
  • Venous blood is dark red; arterial blood is bright red
  • Veins have low pressure; blood flows slowly back into syringe

Difficult Access (DIVA)

  • DIVA score ≥ 4 = high risk of failure on first attempt
  • Risk factors: obesity, dark skin tone, edema, prior chemotherapy
  • Near-infrared visualization (DUALVein) recommended for DIVA ≥ 2
  • Ultrasound guidance for DIVA ≥ 4

Safety

Common IV Complications & How to Respond

Phlebitis

Inflammation of the vein wall. Signs: redness, warmth, pain, and a palpable cord along the vein track.

✅ Prevention: proper site rotation, aseptic technique, appropriate catheter size. Remove catheter immediately if grade ≥ 2.

Infiltration

Non-vesicant fluid leaks into surrounding tissue. Signs: swelling, coolness, pallor, and decreased infusion rate.

✅ Action: stop infusion immediately, remove catheter, elevate the limb, apply warm compress.

Extravasation

Vesicant or irritant fluid leaks into tissue. Potentially causes tissue necrosis if not caught early.

✅ Action: stop infusion STAT, aspirate residual drug, do NOT apply pressure. Notify senior clinician immediately.

Occlusion / Rolling Veins

The most common issue for beginners. Veins "roll" away from the needle due to improper anchoring technique.

✅ Tip: apply traction below the insertion site, advance at a low angle (15–30°). Near-infrared visualization (DUALVein) significantly reduces this error.

Hematoma

Blood pooling under the skin from puncturing through the vein wall or inadequate pressure after removal.

✅ Prevention: maintain firm pressure for ≥ 2 minutes after removal. Do not bend the arm — this reduces pressure on the puncture site.

Catheter-related Infection

Bacterial contamination at insertion site. Risk increases significantly after 72–96 hours of catheter dwell time.

✅ Prevention: strict aseptic technique, CHG-alcohol skin antisepsis, replace per institutional protocol (typically every 72–96 hr).

Evidence-Based Practice

Clinical Guidelines (2024–2025)

Key Recommendations from INS Infusion Therapy Standards (9th Edition, 2024)

  • 1

    Limit insertion attempts to 2 per clinician. After two failed attempts, escalate to a more experienced practitioner or use advanced technology.

  • 2

    Use near-infrared or ultrasound visualization for patients with difficult venous access (DIVA score ≥ 2). Devices like DUALVein directly address this recommendation.

  • 3

    Standardized skin antisepsis with CHG-alcohol (≥ 0.5% chlorhexidine in 70% alcohol) and allow full dry time before insertion.

  • 4

    Daily assessment using the i-DECIDED tool to evaluate catheter necessity, site condition, and patency — helps prevent prolonged unnecessary dwell time.

  • 5

    No routine catheter replacement — replace only when clinically indicated, not on a fixed schedule, provided the site shows no signs of complication.
